Andrew Feenberg resists the fatalist side of Heidegger. He agrees that technology is not neutral, but argues that technologies are socially shaped and politically contestable.

## The central claim

> Technologies are not destiny. They can be redesigned. Enframing (Gestell) is not fate.

Technological design embeds values — what Feenberg calls **technical codes**. Systems can be designed for control or participation, for profit or public good, for surveillance or privacy, for efficiency or care, for hierarchy or democracy.

## Democratic rationalization

Feenberg's alternative: citizens, users, workers, and communities should take part in technological design. This is not anti-technology — it is *democratic* technology. Examples include open-source movements, participatory design, citizen science, and community-controlled infrastructure.

## The political correction

Where Heidegger diagnoses the essence of modern technology as enframing (Gestell), Feenberg replies: yes, but technologies are plural, contested, and open to redesign. The question is not "what is technology?" but "which technology, designed by whom, for whom?"
