The Frankfurt School inherits the Weberian diagnosis of rationalisation and radicalises it into a critique of enlightenment reason.

## The dialectic of enlightenment

In *Dialektik der Aufklärung* (1947), Max Horkheimer and Theodor Adorno set out the thesis that gives the book its title: enlightenment reason itself, by reducing itself to an **instrument for dominating nature**, turns into its opposite and prepares new forms of barbarism. Enlightenment, which promised to free humanity from myth and fear, falls back into myth — the myth of efficiency, of automatic technical progress, of rationalised domination.

## Instrumental reason

The same year, Horkheimer publishes *Eclipse of Reason*, analysing **instrumental reason**: a rationality emptied of any capacity to set ends, become pure technique for fitting means to ends. Instrumental reason is *formally* impeccable — it calculates perfectly how to reach X — but has become *substantially* blind: it can no longer ask whether X is good, just, or desirable.

## The link to Marcuse

The most influential version of this critique for the philosophy of technology is [[Herbert Marcuse — one-dimensional man|Herbert Marcuse's]] in *One-Dimensional Man* (1964): advanced industrial society produces a "one-dimensional man" whose critical thought has been absorbed by a technological apparatus that fuses production, consumption, and culture into a single logic of domination. Marcuse's decisive claim — central to [[Andrew Feenberg — democratic technology|Feenberg]] — is that **modern technology is not neutral**: its very rationality is already a political rationality.

## From diagnosis to legacy

The Frankfurt School's critique thus links [[Max Weber — instrumental rationality and disenchantment|Weber]] (rationalisation and disenchantment) to later critical theories of technology, showing that the threat lies not in the misuse of technique, but in the reduction of reason to pure instrument.
