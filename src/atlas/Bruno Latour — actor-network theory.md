Bruno Latour's actor-network theory (ANT) challenges the idea that only humans act. Agency is distributed across networks of humans and non-humans.

## The central claim

> Action is shared between people and things.

A speed bump "acts" by making drivers slow down. A door-closer "acts" by shutting the door. A database shapes institutional memory. An algorithm shapes visibility and opportunity. Latour does not mean objects have consciousness — he means agency is distributed across networks of humans, devices, rules, institutions, and materials.

## We Have Never Been Modern

Latour's 1991 book argues that modernity pretended to separate nature from culture, object from subject, fact from value — but in practice, hybrids (ozone holes, GMOs, digital platforms) proliferate everywhere. The separation was always a fiction.

## Relevance for technology

ANT dissolves the sharp line between "human users" and "passive tools". Technologies are actors in networks — not because they have intentions, but because they make a difference to the course of action. The question shifts from "what does this tool mean?" to "what does this actor do in this network?"
