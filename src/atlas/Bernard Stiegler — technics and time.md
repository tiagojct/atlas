Bernard Stiegler's monumental *Technics and Time* (1994–) argues that humans are *constitutively* technological — we become human through tools, memory supports, and technical systems.

## The radical thesis

Where Heidegger sometimes suggests technology threatens an original human relation to being, Stiegler argues there is no pre-technical humanity. Tools, writing, archives, images, and machines are not external add-ons to an already-complete humanity. They are the very conditions through which humanity emerges.

## Key concepts

- **Epiphylogenesis:** the evolution of human culture through technical memory supports — writing, photography, cinema, digital archives — that preserve experience beyond individual death.
- **Tertiary retention:** [[Tertiary retention|memory stored outside the body]].
- **Pharmakon:** technology is at once poison and cure — it can capture attention and industrialise desire, but also heal, educate, create, and open new forms of collective intelligence.
- **Proletarianisation:** the loss of knowledge when skills are externalised into machines — not only manual skills, but cognitive and affective ones.

## The contemporary crisis

Digital platforms industrialise attention and desire, producing a crisis of care, memory, and individuation. Stiegler calls for a *pharmacology of attention*: designing technologies that support, rather than destroy, human becoming.
