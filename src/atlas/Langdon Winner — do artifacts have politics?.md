In the essay "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980, reprised in *The Whale and the Reactor*, 1986), Langdon Winner answers yes: artefacts embody political arrangements. He offers two ways to show it.

## First way: intentional political arrangements

Some artefacts are deliberately designed to produce political effects. The famous (and contested) example is the low overpasses of Long Island's parkways, designed by Robert Moses — according to Winner — to bar buses, and so restrict poor and Black populations' access to the beaches.

> Note: this example was empirically contested by Bernward Joerges (1999), who argued the story does not hold up factually. The thesis stands, but the example should be used with care.

## Second way: inherently political technologies

Some technologies **require**, or are strongly compatible with, particular forms of organising power. Nuclear power demands centralised, hierarchical, security-oriented structures; solar power is compatible with decentralisation and local autonomy. The choice of a technical infrastructure is therefore already a political choice.

## Implications

Winner's question shifts the debate: it is not only about *how* a technology is used, but about which forms of life and power are crystallised in the very design of the artefacts.
