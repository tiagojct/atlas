Hubert Dreyfus applied Heidegger's phenomenology directly to artificial intelligence, arguing that human intelligence is not computational but *embodied, situated, and practical*.

## The critique of GOFAI

In *What Computers Can't Do* (1972) and *What Computers Still Can't Do* (1992), Dreyfus criticised early AI for assuming that intelligence is symbolic, rule-based, and detached from context.

## The Heideggerian alternative

Human understanding is not detached calculation. It is **being-in-the-world**: we understand through bodily skill, context, background familiarity, social practice, tacit knowledge, and practical engagement.

A skilled musician, teacher, doctor, or driver does not follow explicit rules. They respond fluidly to meaningful situations — a responsiveness that resists formalisation.

## Contemporary relevance

The critique has evolved, but the central question remains: are we misunderstanding ourselves when we model human intelligence as information processing? Even with large language models, the question is not just whether machines can produce text, but whether intelligence reduces to pattern completion.
