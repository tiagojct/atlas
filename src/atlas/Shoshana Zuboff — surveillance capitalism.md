In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019), Shoshana Zuboff offers the most influential diagnosis of the political economy of data.

## The central claim

> Surveillance capitalism unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioural data … fed into advanced manufacturing processes known as "machine intelligence" and fabricated into prediction products that anticipate what you will do now, soon, and later.

These **prediction products** are traded in **behavioural futures markets**. The ultimate aim is not merely to predict, but to **modify** behaviour at scale.

## Big Other

Zuboff introduces the figure of **Big Other**: a ubiquitous architecture of instrumentation and control that does not rest on totalitarian coercion (Orwell's "Big Brother") but on an "instrumentarian" power — operating through nudges, recommendations, and choice architectures that present themselves as useful, personalised services.

## Behavioural surplus

The concept of **behavioural surplus** names the data that exceed what is needed to improve a product or service and are unilaterally appropriated by firms to feed the prediction markets.

## Morozov's critique

Evgeny Morozov, in a long review ("Capitalism's New Clothes", *The Baffler*, 2019), criticises Zuboff for: treating surveillance as a deviation from a "good" (managerial) capitalism, rather than integrating it into the analysis of capitalist exploitation; overstating the power of "behavioural modification"; and using a term — "surveillance capitalism" — already coined earlier by John Bellamy Foster and Robert McChesney.
