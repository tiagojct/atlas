Yuk Hui argues that Heidegger's philosophy of technology is powerful but too Western. His concept of **cosmotechnics** opens the question of multiple technological futures.

## The critique of universality

Heidegger describes modern technology as enframing (Gestell) — a single, global mode of revealing. Hui asks whether this description assumes one Western history of technology and metaphysics, overlooking how other traditions relate technical activity, moral order, and cosmic order.

## Cosmotechnics

Cosmotechnics names the relation between three terms: *cosmos* (moral and cosmic order), *technics* (making and tools), and *ethics* (the good life). Different cultures configure this relation differently.

A Chinese garden does not impose geometry on nature but works with the landscape, water, and time. Its technique is inseparable from a cosmology. Modern global technology — extraction, automation, standardisation — may not be the only possible technological future.

## What is at stake politically

Hui asks: can there be multiple technological modernities, rather than a single global technological destiny? This is not nostalgia, but a demand for genuine pluralism in how technology relates to nature, ethics, and the cosmos.
