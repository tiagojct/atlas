Peer review is not gatekeeping — it is a craft. It demands reading generously, criticising constructively, and understanding that the goal is a stronger paper, not a perfect one.

## The dimensions of the craft

- **Diagnosis:** identify what is weak — not merely what you disagree with.
- **Specificity:** "the sample is too small" is noise. "A sample of 40 gives you 52% power to detect the effect size you hypothesised" is a review.
- **Tone:** clinical, not personal. The paper is the patient, not the author.
- **Humility:** you may be wrong. Flag concerns; do not issue edicts.

## The decline

Overloaded reviewers, publish-or-perish incentives, and the proliferation of journals have diluted review quality. Yet peer review remains the least-bad quality-control mechanism science has.

## The reviewer's question

Not "is this paper perfect?" but "is the evidence solid enough, and the reasoning transparent enough, to warrant publication — and how could it be stronger?"
