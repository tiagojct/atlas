An "Islamic philosophy of technique" is not a consolidated field, but an emerging area at the crossroads of the history of engineering, the philosophy of science, and the theory of technique.

## Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406)

In the [Muqaddimah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muqaddimah) (1377), [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun) develops a true science of civilisation (*ʿilm al-ʿumrān*) in which crafts and technical arts (*ṣināʿa*) hold a precise place: they flourish with surplus and sedentary life (*ḥaḍara*), are both product and indicator of the degree of civilisation, and decline with the ruin of cities. It is a **sociological theory of technique *avant la lettre***, tying technical development to the density of social cooperation.

## Al-Jazari (1136–1206) and the ḥiyal

The *Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices* (1206) describes fifty automata and water clocks, among them the famous **Elephant Clock**. The genre of the *ḥiyal* (ingenious devices) goes back to the *Kitāb al-Ḥiyal* of the **Banū Mūsā** brothers (9th century).

## Islamic automation (Nadarajan, 2007)

Gunalan Nadarajan, in the essay *Islamic Automation*, proposes that Islamic automata are not an expression of control (as in the Western tradition of robotics) but an expression of **submission** to the rhythms of the machines and, through them, to the divine will — a radically different conception of the relation between human, technique, and cosmos.
