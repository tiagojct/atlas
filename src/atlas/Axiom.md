A proposition accepted without proof. It is not demonstrated — it is assumed as a starting point.

**Euclid's axioms** are the classic case: "through two points passes a single straight line" is not proved, it is accepted. In a formal system, the axioms define what the system "believes" before any reasoning takes place.

The impossibility of proving an axiom is not a weakness — it is its function. Every deductive system rests on undemonstrated propositions; trying to prove everything would collapse into an infinite regress.

## Distinctions

- Unlike the [[Theorem|theorem]], an axiom has no proof.
- Unlike the [[Conjecture|conjecture]], an axiom does not aspire to be proved.
- The [[Postulate|postulate]] is historically equivalent, but carries the nuance of "something asked to be granted".
