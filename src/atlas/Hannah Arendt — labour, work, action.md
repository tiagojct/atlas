[The Human Condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Human_Condition_(Arendt_book)) (1958), by [Hannah Arendt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannah_Arendt), distinguishes three fundamental human activities — with deep implications for how we understand technology.

## The three activities

1. **Labour** — the repetitive activity needed to sustain biological life. Cyclical, it leaves nothing behind and consumes what it produces.
2. **Work** — the fabrication of durable things: buildings, artworks, tools, institutions. It builds a shared, lasting world.
3. **Action** — political and interpersonal activity: speaking, acting, beginning something new among others. The highest form of human freedom.

## The technological threat

Arendt worries that modern technological society increasingly reduces humans to labouring, consuming animals. Life organises itself around production, consumption, maintenance, and process. The shared public world — the space for action and freedom — erodes.

## The question of automation

Automation promises to free humans from labour. But Arendt asks: freed *for what*? If we lose the capacity for action and the care for a shared world, freedom from labour becomes mere freedom to consume.
