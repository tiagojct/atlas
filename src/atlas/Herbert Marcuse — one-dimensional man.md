Herbert Marcuse's *One-Dimensional Man* (1964) argues that advanced industrial societies use technology not only to produce goods but to shape consciousness and erase the capacity to imagine alternatives.

## One-dimensionality

A one-dimensional society is one in which people lose the ability to imagine alternatives to the existing order. Technology, mass media, consumer culture, and administration combine to make the present system appear natural, rational, and inevitable.

## Technology as domination

Marcuse extends Heidegger politically: where Heidegger says technology reveals the world as resource, Marcuse asks *who benefits from this ordering?* Technological rationality serves existing power structures while appearing neutral.

## The trap of comfort

People may feel free because they can choose among products, lifestyles, and entertainments. But their deeper political imagination narrows. Comfort takes the place of freedom.

## The Heideggerian link

Heidegger: technology reveals the world as standing-reserve.
Marcuse: technological society also shapes desire, thought, and politics so that domination appears as comfort, convenience, and progress.
