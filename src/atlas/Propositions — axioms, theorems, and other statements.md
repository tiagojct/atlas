Everyday speech treats these terms as synonyms, which is why they blur together. In epistemology and formal logic each plays a distinct role, defined by its relationship to *proof*.

The central distinction: **axioms are assumed, theorems are proved, conjectures await proof, hypotheses are tested.**

## Map of the concepts

| Concept | Nature | Note |
|---|---|---|
| [[Axiom|Axiom]] | Accepted, unproved | The starting point of a formal system |
| [[Theorem|Theorem]] | Proved from axioms | The proof is what sets it apart |
| [[Postulate|Postulate]] | Accepted, historically "requested" | Today equivalent to an axiom |
| [[Lemma|Lemma]] | Proved, auxiliary | Serves to prove another result |
| [[Corollary|Corollary]] | Proved, direct consequence | The heavy lifting is already done |
| [[Conjecture|Conjecture]] | Unproved, suspected true | A theorem in waiting |
| [[Hypothesis|Hypothesis]] | Assumed for a test or argument | Scientific: falsifiable; mathematical: a local premise |
| [[Thesis|Thesis]] | Defended, not proved | The home of argument, not formal logic |

## A ladder of certainty

From most to least certain:

1. **[[Axiom|Axiom]]** — accepted, unproved
2. **[[Theorem|Theorem]]** — proved from axioms
3. **[[Corollary|Corollary]]** — proved from a theorem
4. **[[Lemma|Lemma]]** — proved, auxiliary to a theorem
5. **[[Conjecture|Conjecture]]** — unproved, suspected true
6. **[[Hypothesis|Hypothesis]]** — assumed for a test or argument
