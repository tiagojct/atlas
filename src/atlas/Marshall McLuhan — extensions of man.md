In [*Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding_Media) (1964), [Marshall McLuhan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan) sets out a principle that upends media theory: "the medium is the message".

## The central claim

> All technologies are extensions of our physical and nervous systems, meant to increase power and speed.

The content a medium carries matters less than the **sensory and social reconfiguration the medium itself performs**. Print does not merely spread texts — it reorganises thought into linear sequences. Television does not merely transmit content — it transforms our sensory relationship with the world.

## Extension and amputation

McLuhan adds the **amputation thesis**: every extension of a faculty is accompanied by a numbing or self-amputation of it. By extending the legs with the wheel, the body "falls asleep" to its capacity to walk long distances. By extending memory with writing and the digital, organic memory atrophies.

This intuition continues the line of [[Ernst Kapp — organ projection|Ernst Kapp]] (the projection of organs) and anticipates contemporary diagnoses of cognitive offloading.

## Limits

The recurring criticism of McLuhan is **technological determinism**: his aphorisms suggest technology inevitably shapes society, underplaying political, cultural, and design agency. Even so, his influence on media ecology ([[Postman — Technopoly|Postman]]) and on media theory is immense.
