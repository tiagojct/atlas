Melville's *Moby-Dick* is not just a novel — it is a treatise on the nature of knowledge, the limits of systematisation, and the obsession with classification.

## The cetology chapters

Ishmael's obsessive cataloguing of whales — by size, by shape, by Linnaean taxonomy — is a satire of encyclopaedic knowledge. He knows the categories are provisional, incomplete, and animated by a deeper mystery. And still he catalogues.

## The white whale

Ahab pursues the whale as a symbol of what cannot be known. The whiteness of the whale is not the absence of colour but the presence of all colours — a metaphor for irreducible complexity. Knowledge pursued as vengeance becomes self-destruction.

## The Pequod

The ship is a floating epistemology: a multicultural, multi-perspectival vessel sailing the unknown. The mates — Starbuck (prudence), Stubb (indifference), Flask (simplicity) — are ways of knowing. None is sufficient alone.
