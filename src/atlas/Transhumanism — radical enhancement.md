Transhumanism, a direct heir of Enlightenment humanism, aims at the radical enhancement of the human through technology, up to the overcoming of the biological condition. Nick Bostrom is also one of its central figures, with works such as *Deep Utopia* (2024).

## A humanist inheritance

Unlike critical posthumanism (Haraway, Hayles), transhumanism does not break with humanism — it **radicalises** it. It starts from the Enlightenment ideal of perfectibility and applies to it the means of contemporary technology: genetic engineering, brain–machine interfaces, life extension.

## Critiques

The philosophical critique of transhumanism turns on four points:

- **Mind–body dualism:** it perpetuates the Cartesian idea that the mind is separable from the organic body.
- **Individualism:** it conceives enhancement as an individual project, ignoring the relational dimension of the human.
- **Inequality:** who will have access to enhancement? It widens the gap between those who can pay and those who cannot.
- **Reducing the human to optimisable information:** it treats the person as a set of parameters to maximise.

Francis Fukuyama called transhumanism "the world's most dangerous idea", seeing in it a threat to the very notion of human dignity grounded in a shared nature.
