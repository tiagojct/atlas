Ernst Kapp (1808–1896) wrote the first book to put "Philosophie" and "Technik" together in its title: *Grundlinien einer Philosophie der Technik* (1877). His central contribution is the principle of **organ projection** (*Organprojektion*).

## The thesis

All technical artefacts are, in their origin, projections of the human body — above all of the hand. The hammer projects the striking fist; the saw projects the teeth; the camera projects the eye; the telegraph projects the nervous system. Technology is an unconscious externalisation of bodily morphology.

## The hand as the primordial organ

For Kapp, the hand is the "instrument of instruments" — the organ that made all other projections possible. The history of technology is therefore a process of *self-recognition*: in making tools, humans project themselves outward, and in contemplating the artefacts, come to know themselves.

## Genealogy

This line runs through Bergson, through Freud (the "prosthetic god" of *Civilization and Its Discontents*), and above all through **[[Marshall McLuhan — extensions of man|Marshall McLuhan]]**, who takes it up and radicalises it in *Understanding Media* (1964): all technologies are extensions of the physical and nervous system.

Kapp thus opens a tradition that thinks technique not as something external to the human, but as a constitutive externalisation of the body and its capacities.
