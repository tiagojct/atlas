The branch of philosophy that examines the nature, meaning, ethics, and existential effects of technology.

## Central questions

- Is technology a neutral tool, or does it shape society and the self?
- Do technologies carry embedded values?
- How do tools change the way we think, perceive, work, and relate to one another?
- Can technological progress make us less free even as it makes us more powerful?
- What does technology reveal — and what does it conceal?

## The central tension

Technology can liberate (medicine, knowledge, communication, assistive devices) and dominate (surveillance, the automation of judgment, the capture of attention, ecological extraction). A mature philosophy of technology avoids both naive optimism and total pessimism.

## Two views

- **Instrumental view:** technology is a neutral means to an end. Humans decide its use.
- **World-shaping view:** technology reorganises power, perception, dependence, habit, and value — not merely serving human purposes but reshaping them.

## An analytic frame

For any technology, ask: what does it extend? What does it reduce? What behaviour does it encourage? Who gains power? Who becomes vulnerable? What values are embedded? Could it have been designed otherwise?
