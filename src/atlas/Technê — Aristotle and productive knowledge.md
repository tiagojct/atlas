In Book VI of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle distinguishes five intellectual virtues — *technê*, *epistêmê*, *phronêsis*, *sophia*, and *nous* — establishing a hierarchy that marks all later philosophy of technology.

## Technê vs epistêmê

**Epistêmê** is knowledge of what cannot be otherwise: necessary truths, of which mathematics is the paradigm. **Technê** (art, craft) is a productive know-how concerned with the contingent — with what *can* be otherwise and depends on an agent to be brought into being.

## A poietic structure

Technê has a *poietic* (productive) structure and is bound up with deliberation. It is not mere routine: the craftsman deliberates about means, adapts to the material, acts with practical intelligence — but its end lies outside the act (the object produced), unlike *praxis*, whose end is the acting itself.

## Why it matters

This distinction is decisive because all modern philosophy of technology returns to it:

- **[[Heidegger — The Question Concerning Technology|Heidegger]]** recovers Greek technê as a mode of *unconcealment* (*alêtheia*) rather than mere instrumental fabrication — hence his insistence that the question of technology is not answered by "it is a means to an end".
- **[[Bernard Stiegler — technics and time|Stiegler]]** charges that philosophy, from its Greek origin, "repressed" technique, treating it as inferior to epistêmê — a thesis he develops in *Technics and Time*.

Technê is not a lesser knowledge: it is the mode of knowing what *is not yet*, and what can come to be only through human action.
