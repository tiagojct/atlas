The view that technologies are shaped by human choices, institutions, conflicts, and values — not by an inevitable internal logic.

## The central claim

> Technologies do not determine their own course. Social groups, economic interests, political decisions, cultural assumptions, and design choices shape what technologies become.

## Examples

- The bicycle: early designs varied enormously (high-wheelers, safety bicycles). The "standard" bicycle emerged through social negotiation, not technical necessity.
- The internet: it could have been more public, decentralised, commercial, military, or surveillance-oriented. Its current form reflects specific historical choices.
- AI alignment: what counts as "aligned" or "safe" is partly a social and political question, not purely a technical one.

## SCOT vs determinism

Where technological determinism says technology drives society, SCOT says society drives technology. A balanced position acknowledges both directions: society shapes technology, and technology then reshapes society.
