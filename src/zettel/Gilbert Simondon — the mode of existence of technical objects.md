Gilbert Simondon (1924–1989) is perhaps the most underrated thinker of twentieth-century philosophy of technology — a situation that has only recently begun to change, through Deleuze, Stiegler, and Hui. His complementary doctoral thesis, *Du mode d'existence des objets techniques* (1958), received a full English translation only in 2017.

## A cultural diagnosis

Simondon begins with a diagnosis: culture treats technical objects with a "primitive xenophobia", either despising them as mere utensils or projecting fantasies onto them (the myth of the robot that revolts). He proposes instead to grant the technical object an ontological status of its own, by studying its genesis.

## Concretisation

The central concept is **concretisation**. Simondon distinguishes:

- **Abstract technical object:** each structural element performs a single, isolated function; conflicts between subsystems demand external compensations (e.g. cooling fins that only dissipate heat).
- **Concrete technical object:** each element is **plurifunctional**; subsystems converge into a coherent unity in which the same element solves several problems at once (e.g. fins that also reinforce the structure).

Concretisation is the gradual reduction of functional antagonisms through the synergistic redistribution of functions. The technical object evolves from the abstract toward the concrete, approaching asymptotically the mode of existence of natural objects.

## Three levels of the technical real

Simondon distinguishes: **element**, **individual**, and **ensemble**. The human should be neither servant nor labourer of the machine, but its "organiser" and the interpreter of the technical ensemble.

## Contemporary relevance

This resonates today in the discussion of AI: treating machine-learning systems neither as mere instruments nor as autonomous agents, but as technical objects with their own mode of existence, co-evolving with us and with their "associated milieu".
