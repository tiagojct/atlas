The deepest question in the philosophy of technology is not "what can technology do?" but **"what kind of beings do we become as our lives are increasingly mediated by technology?"**

## The central dynamic

Humans have always used tools. But some technologies do more than help us complete tasks — they reshape perception, embodiment, memory, our relation to others, our experience of time, and our very sense of what it means to be human.

## What technology changes

- **Perception:** a microscope reveals a hidden world; GPS rewires our sense of place.
- **Embodiment:** a wheelchair extends freedom; a fitness tracker turns the body into metrics.
- **Memory:** digital archives preserve everything — but can weaken the work of recollection.
- **Attention:** platforms compete for it. What you attend to shapes what you become.
- **Identity:** the self splits into a lived self and a datafied self — profile, metrics, archive, performance.
- **Mortality:** technology fights against limits. But are some limits part of what makes a life meaningful?

## The central danger: reduction

Technology works by making reality measurable, repeatable, controllable. But human life exceeds measurement. A person is not only a user, consumer, patient, data point, productivity unit, or cognitive processor. The danger is that partial descriptions become total ones.

## A frame for analysis

Ask of any technology: what does it reveal? What does it conceal? Which capacity does it extend? Which capacity might it weaken? What kind of self does it encourage? What does it make seem normal?
