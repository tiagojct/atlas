In *Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence* (2021), Kate Crawford shifts the analysis from code and algorithms to the **materiality** and **political economy** of artificial intelligence.

## AI as a technology of extraction

The central claim is that AI is an **extractive technology** in three dimensions:

1. **Extraction of minerals from the earth:** lithium for batteries, cobalt for processors, rare earths for screens — AI rests on a resource-intensive supply chain often tied to forced labour and environmental degradation.
2. **Extraction of underpaid labour:** the data annotators who label millions of images, the crowdworkers who train models for pittance wages, the content moderators who scrub datasets of violence and abuse.
3. **Extraction of data:** every action, expression, click, and pause is captured as free raw material for training models.

## Neither artificial nor intelligent

Crawford argues that AI is neither **artificial** (it rests on vast natural resources and human labour) nor **intelligent** (in any autonomous sense). It is an extractive industry that concentrates power and deepens inequality.

## Planetary costs

The book exposes the **planetary costs** of AI: the carbon emissions of training large models, the water consumed cooling data centres, the mining on Indigenous lands. AI has a geography and a geology — it is not a disembodied abstraction.

## Contrast with Bostrom

Where Bostrom focuses on a future, speculative existential risk, Crawford insists on the **present, material harms** of AI: labour exploitation, environmental degradation, the concentration of power. The critique of the existential-risk agenda, in Crawford as in Gebru, is that it diverts attention from the concrete harms already taking place.
