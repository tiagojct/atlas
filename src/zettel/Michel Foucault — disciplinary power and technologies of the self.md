Michel Foucault is not a philosopher of technique in the strict sense, but he is unavoidable for the philosophy of technology, for two fundamental contributions.

## Disciplinary power and the panopticon

In *Discipline and Punish* (1975), Foucault analyses Bentham's **panopticon** as the diagram of a power that operates through visibility, internalised surveillance, and normalisation. The panopticon is not just a prison architecture: it is a model of power in which subjects, knowing themselves potentially watched, internalise discipline and regulate their own behaviour.

This model reappears, transformed, throughout the literature on digital surveillance: platforms need no all-seeing "Big Brother"; it is enough that users know their data are being collected for them to adjust their behaviour.

## Technologies of the self

In his late work, Foucault introduces the concept of **technologies of the self**: the practices by which individuals constitute and transform themselves — care of the self, confession, self-writing, the examination of conscience. These technologies are not imposed from outside; they are practices of self-constitution.

This concept has been productively crossed with postphenomenology (by Verbeek, for instance) to think how concrete technologies — meditation apps, monitoring wearables, digital journals — can be practices of constituting the subject.

## Relevance

Foucault's two contributions offer a frame for thinking technology at once as domination (disciplinary power, biopolitics) and as a practice of freedom (technologies of the self) — a tension that is also Stiegler's, in his concept of the *pharmakon*.
