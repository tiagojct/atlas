The concept of the *pharmakon* — at once remedy and poison — is the foundation of Bernard Stiegler's pharmacology of technique.

## Derrida and writing as pharmakon

In Derrida's reading of Plato's *Phaedrus* (*Plato's Pharmacy*), writing is presented as a *pharmakon*: it supports memory (remedy) and at the same time weakens it (poison), because it dispenses with the inner exercise of recollection. Writing is external, ambivalent — neither pure aid nor pure threat.

## Stiegler: all technique is pharmakon

Stiegler generalises this ambivalence: **all technique is pharmakon**. Every technical prosthesis — from writing to the algorithm — is a condition of the possibility of knowledge and, at the same time, a condition of its possible destruction. There is no harmless technique, just as there is no purely toxic one.

## The digital as cure and poison

Digital technology embodies this ambivalence acutely. It can expand cognition, memory, and relationship — or it can capture attention, automate desire, and proletarianise the mind. Stiegler's pharmacology is neither condemnation nor celebration: it is a **therapeutics**, demanding practices of care (*therapeia*) to tip each pharmakon toward its curative pole.
