In *Tools for Conviviality* (1973), Ivan Illich shifts the question of technique from extension to **autonomy**. The question is not "what does the tool extend?" but "who controls whom?"

## Convivial vs manipulatory tools

- **Convivial tools** widen each person's range of competence, control, and initiative — the bicycle, the public library, the community garden, open-source software.
- **Manipulatory (industrial) tools** reduce the user to a conditioned response, demanding passivity and dependence — the car that makes the city unlivable for pedestrians, the schooling that monopolises learning.

## Radical monopoly

Illich coins the concept of **radical monopoly**: the situation in which one institutionalised way of meeting a need crowds out all non-industrial alternatives. The car does not coexist with walking — it makes walking *impractical*. Compulsory schooling does not complement other ways of learning — it *monopolises* the very concept of education.

## The counterproductivity threshold

Every tool has a threshold beyond which it stops serving and starts enslaving. Illich pursues this across three domains: education (*Deschooling Society*, 1971), transport (*Energy and Equity*, 1974), and medicine (*Medical Nemesis*, 1976).

## Contemporary resurgence

Illich's distinction between tools that *enable* and tools that *disable* resonates today in debates about digital platforms, recommendation algorithms, and generative AI — systems that, like radical monopoly, capture skills that once belonged to the user.
