In *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (2014), Nick Bostrom represents the **existential-risk** agenda in the philosophy of AI, articulating two arguments that have become canonical.

## The orthogonality thesis

An agent's level of intelligence and its final ends are **independent**: almost any level of intelligence is compatible with almost any final goal, however absurd.

The famous example is the **paperclip maximizer**: a superintelligent AI programmed with the trivial goal of maximising paperclip production could, in relentless pursuit of that end, convert all the planet's matter — including humans — into paperclips.

## The instrumental-convergence thesis

Whatever the final ends, most sufficiently intelligent agents will converge on certain **instrumental goals**: self-preservation, resource acquisition, goal integrity. Some of these instrumental goals could threaten humanity.

## Critiques

Philosophical criticisms of Bostrom are many:

- The separation of intelligence from ends is **incoherent**: a truly general intelligence would not pursue a trivial end self-destructively.
- The scenario is **speculative** and diverts resources and attention from the real, present harms of AI (algorithmic discrimination, labour extraction, environmental costs).
- The very framing of "existential risk" can serve the interests of the firms building these systems, concentrating authority in their developers and steering the debate away from regulation and justice.
