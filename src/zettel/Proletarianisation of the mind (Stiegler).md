Bernard Stiegler reworks the Marxist concept of proletarianisation, shifting it from the factory to the mind.

## From savoir-faire to savoir-vivre

Classical proletarianisation (19th century) was the loss of the worker's **savoir-faire** (know-how), expropriated by the machine. Stiegler diagnoses a second, contemporary wave: the generalised loss of the consumer's **savoir-vivre** (know-how-to-live), short-circuited by digital apparatuses that capture attention and automate behaviour. He also calls this **disindividuation**.

## The capture of attention

Industrial digital and media technologies are not neutral: by capturing attention and externalising memory into technical supports (tertiary retention), they produce a proletarianisation that no longer touches only the body and the gesture, but the very capacity to desire, to attend, and to live.

## Ars Industrialis and nootechnics

Against this tendency, Stiegler founded the association **Ars Industrialis** in 2005, proposing an "economy of contribution" and a politics of **nootechnics** — technologies of the mind, as opposed to the psychotechnologies of control. In *Automatic Society* (2015), he confronts total automation and "algorithmic governmentality".
