The debate between two fundamental ways of understanding what technology is.

## The instrumental view

> Technology is a neutral means to an end. Humans decide whether to use it well or badly.

This is the everyday view. A hammer can build or destroy. A smartphone can educate or distract. AI can help doctors or spread disinformation. The whole moral weight falls on human intention.

Heidegger calls this view *correct but shallow*. It overlooks how technologies shape the field of possible actions, habits, values, and forms of life.

## The world-shaping view

> Technologies are not neutral. They reorganise power, perception, dependence, habit, and value.

A city built around the car does not merely "offer" cars as tools — it changes where people live, how public space is arranged, how children move, what counts as independence. Technology becomes part of a social order.

## The danger of the instrumental view

If we believe technology is just a neutral instrument, we fail to notice how it has already shaped our desires, assumptions, language, and sense of reality. We take ourselves to be masters — and that very belief is part of the danger.
