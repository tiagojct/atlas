[Byung-Chul Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byung-Chul_Han) extends the themes of [[Heidegger — The Question Concerning Technology|Heidegger]] into digital life. In *The Burnout Society* (2010) and *The Transparency Society* (2012), he argues that modern power no longer works mainly by repression — we exploit ourselves through self-optimisation.

## The achievement subject

The modern subject is no longer Foucault's disciplined, obedient one. It is the **achievement subject**: always improving, producing, visible, communicating, comparing, optimising. Freedom becomes compulsion: *you can*, therefore *you must*.

## Digital intensification

Digital technology accelerates this: social media produce a culture of transparency, exposure, immediacy, and performance. Everything must be visible and circulable. The self becomes a project of constant management.

## The loss of depth

For Han, human depth requires a degree of opacity, silence, distance, and negativity — what he calls *the negative*. But digital culture pushes everything toward visibility and positivity. We lose the capacity for genuine otherness, contemplation, and eros.

## Connections

Where Heidegger worries that technology makes everything *available and orderable*, Han says digital society makes the self *always-visible and always-performing*. The existential question shifts: what happens to the human soul when everything must be transparent, productive, and available?
